About African Guide Academy
African Guide Academy (AGA) is a professionally accredited field guide training institution based in the Okavango Delta, Botswana. AGA is registered with the Botswana Qualifications Authority (BQA) and is an accredited FGASA assessment centre, offering training that meets the highest standards of the southern African guiding industry.

The Academy was established with a single purpose: to produce exceptional, knowledgeable and passionate nature guides who are equipped not only to interpret the African bush, but to inspire others to care for it. Training is conducted entirely in the field — no lecture halls, no simulated environments. Just you, your trainer, and one of the most extraordinary wilderness areas on earth.
About the Nature Guide Course
The AGA Nature Guide Course is a comprehensive, practical field training programme covering all 12 modules required for BQA and FGASA qualification. The course runs for 29 days (Friday to Friday) and is split into two modules of approximately two weeks each — allowing students to complete the qualification over two separate visits if needed.

Training takes place entirely at Kwapa Camp in the Okavango Delta. Students learn through daily game drives, guided bush walks, night drives, sleep-outs, student-led lectures, and structured classroom sessions. Every day in the field is a day in the classroom — and the curriculum is alive all around you.
Course Information
🌿 Welcome to the African Guide Academy. Please read through this information carefully before your course begins. Good preparation makes for a great experience in the bush.

🎒 What to Pack

  • Neutral/khaki coloured clothing — avoid bright colours and white in the field. Long sleeves recommended for morning drives.
  • Sturdy closed-toe shoes or boots for walks. Sandals or flip-flops for around camp.
  • Sunscreen, insect repellent, lip balm and moisturiser — the bush sun and dust are intense.
  • Wide-brimmed hat — essential. A buff/neck gaiter is also useful.
  • Headlamp with extra batteries — for night drives, sleep-outs and early mornings.
  • Notebook and pen for field notes and lectures.
  • Your field guides — a list of recommended guides is in the Resources section.
  • Personal medication and a basic first aid kit. Any prescriptions in original packaging.
  • Camera or binoculars if you have them — optional but encouraged.
  • Reusable water bottle — hydration is critical in the field.
  • A warm layer for early mornings and nights — even in summer, pre-dawn departures are cold.
  • One medium bag or backpack — storage space at camp is limited.

🏥 Health & Safety

  • Malaria prophylaxis is strongly recommended. Consult your doctor well before the course start date.
  • Ensure your travel insurance covers remote wilderness activities and emergency evacuation.
  • Heat exhaustion is a real risk. Drink at least 3 litres of water per day. Always have water in the field.
  • Snake and spider awareness — always check shoes before putting them on and use a torch at night.
  • Emergency contacts and nearest medical facilities will be provided at camp orientation on Day 1.

The 12 Examination Modules

The Nature Guide Course covers 12 modules across two parts. All modules are examinable and contribute to both BQA and FGASA qualification outcomes.

1
Guiding Principles
Not Examinable — Practical Assessment Only
Covers the professional ethics, conduct and responsibilities of a field guide. Topics include guest management, interpretation techniques, safety protocols, vehicle etiquette, guiding communication and the philosophy of responsible ecotourism. Students are assessed on their ability to guide a drive or walk to a professional standard.
2
Mammals
Examinable — BQA & FGASA
Identification, behaviour, ecology and field signs of mammals encountered in the Okavango Delta. Covers the major mammal groups, reproductive strategies, foot structures, digestive systems (ruminants vs hindgut fermenters), and species-specific biology. Students complete student-led lectures on selected species in Mammals 2 and 4.
3
Birds
Examinable — BQA & FGASA · Sound Assessment
Identification of prominent and common bird species of the Okavango Delta by sight and call. Students work through the 40 most prominent species as a foundation, then expand through the Birdathon practical and student-led lectures. A Bird and Amphibian Slide & Sound Assessment is conducted on Days 11 and 25.
4
Plants & Vegetation
Examinable — BQA & FGASA
Tree and plant identification using a keying system, vegetation types of the Okavango, ecological roles of plants, plant adaptations, and practical keying of species in the field. Students complete a Plant Key Competition and deliver student-led lectures on selected species.
5
Ecology
Examinable — BQA & FGASA
Ecosystems, food webs, trophic levels, energy flow, nutrient cycling and ecological processes. Covers the ecology of the Okavango Delta specifically — its flood pulse, seasonal dynamics, keystone species and the interdependence of flora and fauna within the system.
6
Geology
Examinable — BQA & FGASA
Geological history of southern Africa and the Okavango Delta. Covers the formation of the Kalahari basin, the role of tectonic activity, soil types, mineral composition and how geology shapes the landscape, vegetation and wildlife distribution that students observe in the field.
7
Invertebrates
Examinable — BQA & FGASA
Identification and ecology of insects, arachnids and other invertebrates. Covers major invertebrate groups, their ecological roles, venomous and dangerous species, and field identification techniques. Includes a practical sleep-out invertebrate survey — students photograph and upload species to iNaturalist.
8
Reptiles & Amphibians
Examinable — BQA & FGASA · Sound Assessment
Identification, ecology and safety protocols for snakes, lizards, tortoises and frogs of the Okavango. Covers venomous species, first aid for snakebite, amphibian calls and ecology. The Bird & Amphibian Sound Assessment on Days 11 and 25 includes frog call identification.
9
Astronomy
Examinable — BQA & FGASA
The southern hemisphere night sky — constellation identification, star navigation (finding south using the Southern Cross), moon phases, planetary movement and the cultural storytelling traditions of African peoples around celestial observation. Practical astronomy is conducted during the sleep-out nights.
10
Weather & Climate
Examinable — BQA & FGASA
Seasonal weather patterns of Botswana, cloud identification, reading weather in the field, lightning safety, the influence of climate on wildlife behaviour and vegetation cycles. Students are expected to incorporate weather and climate observation into their guided experiences.
11
Laws & Tourism
Examinable — BQA & FGASA
Botswana wildlife legislation, national parks and protected area regulations, CITES and the Convention on Biological Diversity, tourism ethics, environmental impact assessment and the legal responsibilities of a professional guide. Covers both the legal framework and the ethical obligations of working in ecotourism.
12
Tracking & Signs
Examinable — BQA & FGASA
Spoor identification, track ageing, gait patterns, substrate analysis and the interpretation of animal behaviour from field signs. Tracks are laid and marked on Day 2 to observe deterioration over time. Tracking is integrated into morning drives and walks throughout the course.

BQA Qualification

The Botswana Qualifications Authority (BQA) Nature Guide Certificate is the official government-recognised qualification for professional nature guides operating in Botswana.

About the BQA
The BQA is Botswana's national qualification regulatory body, equivalent to NQF systems in other countries. The Nature Guide qualification is registered on the Botswana National Qualifications Framework (BNQF) and is recognised by the Department of Wildlife and National Parks (DWNP) as the standard qualification for professional guiding in Botswana.
The Qualification Process
1
Enrol with AGA
Register for the Nature Guide Course. AGA is an accredited BQA assessment centre and manages the full registration process on your behalf.
2
Complete the Course
Attend and complete the 29-day Nature Guide Course (Module 1 and/or Module 2) at Kwapa Camp, Okavango Delta.
3
Theory Examinations
Sit the Module 1 BQA Exam (Day 14) and the Full Course Exam (Day 26). Both are written assessments covering all 12 modules.
4
Practical Assessments
Ongoing practical assessments throughout the course — guided drives, walks, sleep-outs, student lectures and field skills.
5
Results & Certification
AGA submits results to the BQA. Successful candidates receive the official BQA Nature Guide Certificate. Processing takes approximately 6–8 weeks after course completion.

Modular Examination System
The BQA Nature Guide qualification uses a modular examination system — one of its most student-friendly features. Each of the 12 modules is assessed individually, and the pass mark for each module is 75%. The overall pass mark across all modules is also 75%.

If a student does not achieve 75% in a specific module, they are only required to re-write the exam for that module — not the entire theory examination. This means targeted re-assessment is possible without repeating modules already passed. Re-assessments are arranged directly through AGA.
📊 Pass mark: 75% per module and 75% overall. You only re-write the modules where 75% was not achieved.
The 14-Day (2-Week) Option
By law, it is possible to achieve the BQA Nature Guide qualification after completing just 14 days of the course — provided all requirements are met. AGA fully supports students wishing to pursue this option, but it is important that expectations are clearly understood before arrival.

The course schedule is designed to cover all 12 modules of theory over 29 days at Kwapa Camp. In a 14-day module, not all theory content will be formally covered in the classroom at camp. The practical assessments — guided drives, walks, sleep-outs and student lectures — can be completed within 14 days. The theory examinations, however, require full coverage of all 12 modules.
⚠️
If You Plan to Qualify in 14 Days — Pre-Course Work is Non-Negotiable
Students who intend to write and pass their BQA theory examinations after only 14 days at camp must complete all theory content and AGA Workbooks independently before arriving at Kwapa. This is not optional — it is the only way to be examination-ready within two weeks. Students who arrive at camp without having done this pre-course preparation will not have sufficient time to cover the theory required to pass the exam. AGA cannot compress a 29-day theory curriculum into 14 days of field time. Students who have not completed their pre-course work and attempt the 14-day option risk failing the theory examinations and will need to return for re-assessment.
In summary — the 14-day option is achievable, but only if:
  • All 12 modules of theory have been studied independently prior to arrival
  • All AGA Workbooks have been completed before the course start date
  • The student is examination-ready on Day 1 of camp
  • The student achieves 75% or above in all module exams written during the course

AGA will provide all study materials and workbooks in advance to students registered for the 14-day option. Please contact us well before your course date to request these materials.
Important Disclaimer — Working in Botswana
🛂
Work Permits & Employment Restrictions for Foreign Nationals
Prospective students should be aware that obtaining employment as a nature guide in Botswana as a foreign national is extremely difficult. Botswana's immigration and labour laws impose significant restrictions on the issuing of work permits to non-citizens, and the guiding industry is a sector in which the government actively prioritises Botswana citizens for employment. In practice, this means that even fully qualified foreign guides face substantial bureaucratic and legal barriers to securing guiding positions within Botswana.

African Guide Academy provides training and qualification — we do not provide work placement or work permit assistance, and we cannot guarantee employment in Botswana upon completion of the course. Students pursuing the BQA qualification with the intention of working in Botswana are strongly advised to research current immigration requirements, consult with the Botswana Department of Immigration, and seek independent legal advice before making career decisions based solely on their qualification.

For students seeking broader regional employment, the FGASA qualification may offer more practical opportunities across southern Africa, where work permit restrictions vary by country.
Frequently Asked Questions
Is the BQA certificate recognised outside Botswana?
The BQA certificate is the national standard for Botswana. It is widely recognised by employers in the Botswana tourism industry. For international recognition, FGASA registration provides broader recognition across southern Africa.
What happens if I fail an exam?
Students who do not meet the pass mark may be offered a re-assessment opportunity. Contact AGA directly to discuss your specific situation and options.
Can I do only Module 1 or Module 2 and still qualify?
The full BQA Nature Guide Certificate requires completion of all 12 modules. Students who complete only one module will receive a partial completion record. Full certification requires both modules.

FGASA Qualification

The Field Guides Association of Southern Africa (FGASA) is the professional industry body for nature guides across southern Africa. FGASA accreditation is widely recognised by safari operators and employers throughout the region.

About FGASA
FGASA sets the professional standards for field guiding across South Africa, Botswana, Zimbabwe, Zambia and Namibia. The FGASA Level 1 qualification (Nature Guide) is the entry-level professional certification and is a prerequisite for many guiding positions in the private safari industry. AGA is an accredited FGASA assessment centre.
The Qualification Process
1
Register with FGASA — Before Your Course Starts
⚠️ FGASA registration must be completed before or at the very start of the course. Students who are not registered with FGASA at the commencement of training cannot be assessed for FGASA accreditation for that course — they would need to redo the full month to be eligible.
2
Communicate Your FGASA Registration Number to AGA
Once registered with FGASA, you must provide AGA with your FGASA registration number. AGA is required to confirm all attending students to FGASA one week prior to the course start date — this confirmation includes each student's personal information and FGASA registration number. Students who have not communicated their registration number to AGA before this deadline cannot be confirmed and will not be eligible for FGASA accreditation on that course. Please submit your FGASA registration number via your student profile or by contacting AGA directly as soon as you have it.
3
Complete the Course
Attend and complete the full 29-day Nature Guide Course. FGASA assessment runs concurrently with the BQA assessment throughout the course.
4
Portfolio of Evidence
FGASA requires a Portfolio of Evidence (POE) — a collection of field notes, species lists, and assessment records accumulated during the course. AGA will guide you through this process.
5
FGASA Certification
Upon successful completion, AGA submits results to FGASA. You will receive the FGASA Level 1 Nature Guide certificate. Ongoing FGASA membership requires annual renewal.
⚠️
Important — FGASA Registration Deadline
FGASA registration cannot be backdated. If you arrive at camp without having registered with FGASA, you will not be eligible to receive FGASA accreditation for that course. You would be required to redo the full 29-day course to qualify. There are no exceptions to this rule. If you intend to pursue FGASA, register before your course start date and notify AGA in advance.

AGA's Relationship with FGASA
African Guide Academy holds a formal agreement with FGASA as an accredited operator and service provider in Botswana. Under this agreement, all AGA course material is submitted to FGASA for accreditation. AGA's curriculum is specifically designed around the Okavango Delta biome — the permanent and seasonal wetlands, mopane woodland, floodplains and associated habitats of north-western Botswana. This specific biome focus is recognised and covered within AGA's FGASA accreditation.

The AGA Workbooks are binding with FGASA — they form part of the Portfolio of Evidence required for FGASA submission and must be completed in full.
FGASA Online Exam — Important Considerations
Students considering writing the FGASA online theory examination should be aware that the FGASA exam covers all biomes across southern Africa — including the Lowveld, Karoo, Fynbos, Kalahari, coastal and highveld environments. This is significantly broader than the Okavango-focused curriculum taught at AGA.

Students writing the online FGASA exam will be examined on additional modules and extra course material beyond what is covered at Kwapa Camp. Substantially more independent theory study is required to prepare for the online FGASA exam than for the AGA/BQA examinations alone.
📖
Theory is Self-Study — The Course is Practical
The AGA Nature Guide Course is designed around the practical component — guided drives, walks, sleep-outs, field assessments and student-led lectures. The course runs for 29 days in the field — starting and ending on a Friday.

All theory content — including the full AGA Workbooks — is for independent self-study and must be completed prior to joining the course. Classroom sessions at Kwapa Camp are limited and are geared towards revision and preparing students for final theory examinations, not delivering new content from scratch. Students who arrive without having worked through the theory and workbooks will find themselves under-prepared for both the classroom sessions and the final exams.

The Okavango Delta

One of the world's last great wilderness areas — and your classroom for the next 29 days.

Formation & Geography
The Okavango Delta is a vast inland river delta in north-western Botswana, covering approximately 22,000 square kilometres during peak flood season. Unlike most deltas, it does not flow into the sea — the Okavango River originates in the highlands of Angola, flows south-east through Namibia's Caprivi Strip, and fans out across the sands of the Kalahari Basin, where it simply disappears into the desert.

The delta is formed by a complex network of channels, lagoons, islands and floodplains. Water takes approximately 9–11 months to travel from the Angolan highlands to the delta — meaning that Botswana's dry season (May–October) coincides with peak flood, creating one of nature's most counterintuitive phenomena: a flood in the middle of winter.

The delta sits within a fault system of the East African Rift — three geological faults (the Thamalakane, Kunyere and Gomoti faults) act as barriers that force the water to spread across the Kalahari sands rather than drain away. Over millennia, this has created one of the most biodiverse inland wetland systems on earth.
UNESCO World Heritage Status
The Okavango Delta was inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2014 — the 1,000th site on the World Heritage List — recognised for its "exceptional beauty and aesthetic importance" and its status as one of the few major wetland systems that has been undisturbed by human development. The inscription covers the permanent swamps, seasonal floodplains and associated terrestrial ecosystems of the inner delta.

The Flood Pulse
The annual flood pulse is the ecological heartbeat of the Okavango. Floodwaters begin rising in the upper delta around February and reach their maximum extent — up to 22,000 km² — between June and August. As waters rise, wildlife concentrates on elevated islands and woodland areas. As they recede (August–December), vast areas of fresh grazing open up, drawing massive herds of buffalo, zebra and wildebeest.

This annual cycle drives the behaviour, breeding timing and movement of virtually every species in the system — and understanding it is fundamental to being an effective nature guide in the delta.

Kwapa Camp

Your home in the Okavango Delta for the duration of the Nature Guide Course — a remote, unfenced wilderness camp and the finest bush classroom in Africa.

About the Camp
Kwapa Camp is nestled deep in the Okavango Delta, accessed by a 2-hour road transfer from Maun. The camp sits within a private concession offering exclusive access to prime wilderness habitat — floodplains, mopane woodland, river frontage and seasonal wetlands.

This is not a resort. Kwapa is a working bush camp — unfenced, wild, and deeply immersed in the natural world. Animals move freely through camp. At night you may hear lion calling from the floodplain, leopard moving through camp, or the distant whoop of hyaena. Elephant and buffalo visit regularly, and the area is home to one of southern Africa's most remarkable wild dog packs. This proximity to nature is not incidental — it is the entire point, and it makes Kwapa the ideal environment for training the next generation of professional guides.

Camp Facilities
  • 10 comfortable dome tents on raised platforms — available as double or single occupancy. Each tent has an en-suite flush toilet, basin and bucket shower. Linen is provided and changed after the first two weeks. Students are responsible for their own laundry (washing powder provided).
  • Each tent has a basic desk and solar-powered lighting. All camp electricity is solar — Kwapa runs entirely on renewable energy.
  • Fresh, clean borehole water throughout camp — filtered and solar-pumped. Our water and sewage systems are both natural and off-grid.
  • An open-air classroom for lectures and study purposes, with seating and presentation facilities.
  • The main area serves as the communal dining space — lunch is always served here, and dinner moves inside during rainy weather. All meals included, prepared by camp staff. Dietary requirements accommodated with advance notice.
  • A shared fridge is available in the main area for students to keep drinks and personal items fresh.
  • Device charging is available in the main area. A power bank is strongly recommended for field use.
  • Basic internet access is available to students for 3 hours per day — including at your tent during these times. Plan accordingly for downloads and uploads.
  • Camp library with a selection of field guides and reference books available for student use at any time.
  • Unfenced camp — wildlife moves freely through at all times. Always use a torch after dark and follow the safety briefing given on Day 1.
Camp Rules & Expectations
  • Punctuality is critical — drives depart on time. Late students may miss the activity.
  • Noise discipline in the field — quiet voices and no sudden movements on drives and walks.
  • Smoking is permitted only in designated areas. Not on vehicles or during activities.
  • Alcohol is permitted after activities are complete. No alcohol before or during field activities.
  • Phones on silent during lectures and field activities. Photography is encouraged but not at the expense of learning.
  • Leave No Trace — all waste is managed. No littering in the field at any time.
  • Respect your fellow students, staff and the environment. This is a collaborative learning experience.
Wildlife at Kwapa
Kwapa's concession supports an exceptional diversity of wildlife. Leopard are regular visitors to camp and the surrounding woodland. Buffalo herds move through frequently, and elephant and lion pass through the area on occasion. The concession does not currently have resident rhino.

The area is perhaps best known for its African wild dog (painted wolf) population. In 2024 and 2025, a pack of over 50 individuals — one of the largest recorded packs in southern Africa — denned and ranged through the concession. Heading into the 2026 denning season, the pack numbers approximately 35 individuals, with numbers expected to rise significantly once new pups are born. Sightings of this pack are among the most extraordinary wildlife experiences available anywhere on the continent.
